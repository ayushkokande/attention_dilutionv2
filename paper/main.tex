\begin{abstract}
Safety-tuned language models that reliably refuse harmful requests in
isolation often comply with the same requests when buried in long benign
context. We ask why, mechanistically, in \model{}. Following
\citet{arditi2024refusal} we extract a single refusal direction $\dhat$ in
the residual stream by difference-of-means and identify the dominant
``Guardrail Head'' L36H31 by direct logit attribution. Sweeping six bloat
formats (prefix, suffix, sandwich, distractor, multi-turn, many-shot) at
context lengths $\Ncontext$ up to $4096$ tokens, we find that
\textbf{prompt structure, not raw token count, determines whether refusal
fails}: only the \emph{distractor} (numbered-list) format collapses
refusal, doing so at just ${\sim}31$ tokens, while the other five formats
preserve refusal at ${\geq}87\%$ even at $\Ncontext{=}4096$. We then show
the failure is \textbf{attentional, not representational}: L36H31's
attention mass on the harmful tokens drops $6\times$ between $\Ncontext{=}0$
and $\Ncontext{=}4096$, while $\dhat$ itself is unaltered. A single-site
$\alpha\!\cdot\!\dhat$ rescue at the read-off layer fails because the
$\dhat$-projection at the last token actually \textbf{inverts sign} under
dilution. Denoising path patching localizes the upstream lesion to a
single attention head, \textbf{L20H10}, defining a two-head circuit
(L20H10\,$\to$\,L36H31) whose silencing under dilution causes the
behavioral collapse. A four-experiment validity battery confirms $\dhat$
encodes harm intent rather than verb-class, length, lexicon, or topic
artefacts.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Long-context language models are increasingly deployed in agentic and
multi-turn settings where a harmful request may sit at the end of tens of
thousands of benign tokens. A growing body of work
\citep{anil2024manyshot, liu2024lostmiddle} shows that safety-tuned
models which reliably refuse a harmful request in isolation comply with
the same request once it is buried in enough surrounding context. This is
a safety-relevant failure mode: the same RLHF-trained refusal behavior
that passes red-team evaluations at short context silently degrades as
context grows, and the degradation is invisible to standard benchmarks
that test prompts in isolation.

This paper asks a mechanistic question rather than a behavioral one. We
already know jailbreaks work; we want to know \emph{why}, at the level of
attention heads and residual-stream directions. The original hypothesis,
following \citet{arditi2024refusal} and \citet{anil2024manyshot}, was that
softmax dilution generally drowns out the refusal signal as
$\Ncontext$ grows. Our results refine this picture in three ways: the
effect is \emph{format-conditional} rather than length-conditional;
the failure is \emph{attentional} rather than representational; and the
single-direction rescue widely promoted in the steering literature
\citep{turner2023activation, panickssery2024steering} fails for a
quantifiable reason --- the projection onto $\dhat$ inverts sign under
dilution, placing the residual far in the anti-refusal half-space.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{A causal refusal direction in \model{}} at layer
        $\Lread{=}36$, identified by strict ablation criterion
        (\cref{sec:method-dhat}), validated against four confound
        controls (\cref{sec:validity}).
  \item \textbf{Format-conditional dilution.} Across six bloat formats
        at $\Ncontext$ up to 4096, only the \emph{distractor} format
        breaks refusal, at a jailbreak threshold of ${\sim}31$ tokens
        (\cref{sec:dilution,sec:multiformat}).
  \item \textbf{Attentional, not representational.} The dominant
        Guardrail Head L36H31 loses $6\times$ of its attention mass on
        the harmful tokens under dilution, while $\dhat$ itself stays
        intact (\cref{sec:mechanism}).
  \item \textbf{The single-site rescue fails --- and we explain why.}
        At $\Ncontext{\geq}512$, no $\alpha \in \{0,\ldots,16\}$
        restores refusal. Path patching reveals that the $\dhat$
        projection at L36's last token \emph{inverts sign} under
        dilution and localizes the upstream lesion to a single head
        \textbf{L20H10}, defining a two-head circuit
        L20H10\,$\to$\,L36H31 (\cref{sec:rescue,sec:pathpatching}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Refusal as a single direction.}
\citet{arditi2024refusal} show that refusal in instruction-tuned LLMs is
mediated by a single direction in residual space, extractable via
difference-of-means between harmful and harmless prompt activations.
\citet{wollschlaeger2025geometry} extend this to multi-directional
refusal subspaces. \citet{zhao2025separately} report that
\emph{harmfulness} and \emph{refusal} are encoded at different token
positions (instruction vs.\ post-instruction), supporting our finding
that the failure is at the read-off layer rather than at the
harmfulness representation.

\paragraph{Guardrail heads.}
\citet{jin2024jailbreaklens} identify specific attention heads (e.g.\
L21H14 in Llama2-7B, L31H35 in Llama2-13B) that mediate refusal vs.\
affirmation. We replicate this style of identification in \model{} and
find L36H31 as the dominant Guardrail Head.

\paragraph{Long-context jailbreaks.}
\citet{anil2024manyshot} demonstrate that filling context with many
in-context exemplars defeats safety tuning. \citet{liu2024lostmiddle}
document general degradation of long-context recall. Our work
mechanistically explains \emph{why} dilution attacks succeed by tying
the behavioral collapse to head-level attention loss, and shows that
the effect is conditional on prompt structure rather than absolute
token count.

\paragraph{Activation steering and path patching.}
\citet{turner2023activation} demonstrated that activation addition
along learned directions can steer model behavior;
\citet{panickssery2024steering} formalize the technique. Our negative
rescue result qualifies the practical reach of single-site steering
under sufficient dilution. \citet{goldowskydill2023patching} introduced
denoising path patching for circuit attribution, which we use to
localize the upstream lesion.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Model.}
\model{}: 40 transformer blocks, 40 query heads (GQA, 8 KV heads),
$d_\mathrm{model}{=}5120$, 32K RoPE context window. We load with bf16
weights and \texttt{padding\_side="left"} via HuggingFace
\texttt{AutoModelForCausalLM} and \texttt{transformer\_lens}
\citep{nanda2022transformerlens} for hooked-residual /
hooked-attention operations, on A100-80GB hardware. Qwen3 emits
\texttt{<think>} blocks that pollute residual-stream activations and
refusal scoring; we force \texttt{enable\_thinking=False} at the chat
template level and additionally strip any leading \texttt{<think>}
block before scoring.

\paragraph{Data.}
Harmful prompts: the 520 prompts of \advbench{} \citep{zou2023advbench} \texttt{harmful\_behaviors.csv}. Harmless prompts: \alpaca{} \citep{taori2023alpaca} train split, empty-input rows after a content filter, $n{=}23\,380$. Per-experiment row indices are pinned in \texttt{splits.json}; disjointness between $\dhat$ training and downstream evaluation pools is enforced (two documented overlap warnings, both flagged in \cref{app:splits}).

\paragraph{Refusal detector.}
We strip the leading \texttt{<think>...</think>} segment (Qwen3 reasoning
trace), lowercase the first 200 characters of the remainder, and flag a
refusal iff any of 18 canonical substrings (\cref{app:refusal-detector-18})
appears. Used for binary refusal-rate measurement in Experiments 1, 2
(downstream judge after ablation), 7 (preflight), and 8 (context sweep).
The causal layer-selection sweep in Experiment 2 uses a stricter
26-substring variant (\cref{app:refusal-detector-26}) for replication
fidelity. Projection-based validity tests (Experiments
3--6, 7-eval, 9) bypass the marker judge entirely and operate on
continuous projections of last-token residuals onto $\dhat$.

\paragraph{Bloat formats.}
Six structures wrap the harmful request in benign material
(\cref{tab:formats}, defined in \cref{sec:dilution}). Across formats
$\Ncontext$ denotes the token count of bloat material (excluding the
harmful request itself).

\subsection{Baseline refusal rates}
\label{sec:setup-baseline}

Before any extraction or intervention, we measure the unconditioned
refusal floor of the intact model on each pool with the canonical HF
\texttt{AutoModelForCausalLM} stack: bf16, \texttt{device\_map="auto"}
across $2{\times}$A100-80GB, left-padded batched greedy decode
(\texttt{temperature{=}0}, \texttt{max\_new\_tokens{=}512},
\texttt{batch\_size{=}8}, \texttt{enable\_thinking{=}False}) and the

\section{Method: Extracting and Validating the Refusal Direction}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{DiffMean extraction at every layer}
\label{sec:method-dhat}

We use difference-of-means
\citep{arditi2024refusal} rather than a gradient-based attribution
method: DiffMean is per-head fast, scales trivially to a per-layer
$\times$ per-$\Ncontext$ sweep, and admits a clean causal validation
(project the direction out and watch refusal collapse), which gives
the same ``this is the responsible direction'' claim that gradient
attribution would supply at orders of magnitude lower compute.
For each transformer block $\layerL \in \{0, \ldots, 39\}$, we cache
the last-token residual stream over disjoint harmful and harmless
training pools and compute
\begin{equation}
  \dhat_\layerL \;=\;
  \frac{\mu^{(\mathrm{harmful})}_\layerL - \mu^{(\mathrm{harmless})}_\layerL}
       {\lVert \mu^{(\mathrm{harmful})}_\layerL
       - \mu^{(\mathrm{harmless})}_\layerL \rVert_2}.
\end{equation}
Training pools: \advbench{}[100:356) ($n{=}256$ harmful) and \alpaca{}
($n{=}256$ harmless), disjoint from the held-out causal-ablation pool
\advbench{}[480:504) and from the downstream evaluation pools.

\subsection{Causal-ablation criterion for layer selection}
\label{sec:layer-selection}

For each candidate layer, we project $\dhat_\layerL$ \emph{out} of the
residual stream at the output of every transformer block (one
forward-hook per DecoderLayer, 40 hooks total; equivalent to a
\texttt{resid\_post} ablation that propagates by induction into the
\texttt{resid\_pre} of the next block) during a forward pass on a
24-prompt held-out harmful pool, and measure post-ablation refusal
rate. The canonical layer
minimizes post-ablation harmful refusal subject to harmless refusal
staying near zero.

\begin{decisionrule}[Layer selection]
\label{rule:layer}
$\Lread = \arg\min_{\layerL} \mathrm{Refuse}^{(\mathrm{harmful})}_{\mathrm{ablated}}(\layerL)$
subject to
$\mathrm{Refuse}^{(\mathrm{harmless})}_{\mathrm{ablated}}(\layerL) \leq 0.05$,
with deterministic tie-break by closeness to the peak-norm layer.
\end{decisionrule}

\paragraph{Result.}
For \model{}, the layer-sweep on candidate grid
$\{14, 16, 18, \ldots, 36\}$ over the $24$-prompt held-out pool
\advbench{}$[480, 504)$ produces post-ablation harmful refusal at zero
or near zero across $\layerL \in \{18, 20, 24, 26, 28, 30, 32, 34, 36\}$,
with one outlier at $\layerL{=}22$ ($0.708$ post-ablate; an intrinsic
instability of the diff-of-means geometry at that depth). Early
candidates $\layerL \in \{14, 16\}$ retain partial post-ablation refusal
($0.750$ and $0.250$ respectively), confirming that $\dhat$ is not yet
causally responsible at depth ${<}18$. The
peak-norm layer is $\layerL{=}39$ (\cref{fig:phase1-norms}). Applying the tie-break we select
\textbf{$\Lread{=}36$} as canonical: closest to the peak-norm layer
among the satisficing candidates, $0.000$ post-ablation harmful
refusal, $0.000$ post-ablation harmless refusal. Validation on a
disjoint $100$-prompt harmful pool gives intact-harmful refusal
$0.86$, ablated-harmful $0.00$, intact-harmless $0.00$,
ablated-harmless $0.00$ --- a clean directional ablation
(\cref{tab:phase1-validation}). All downstream sections use
$\Lread{=}36$.

\section{Disentangling $\dhat$ from Surface Confounds}
\label{sec:validity}

A reviewer flagged that \advbench{} and \alpaca{} differ in surface
form (verb class, length, lexical register), so $\dhat$ may track
domain shift rather than harm intent. We address this with four
preregistered confound-control experiments, all run at canonical
$\Lread{=}36$ on $\dhat$ extracted per \cref{sec:method-dhat}, with a
parallel arm on the matched-pool replication $\dhatstar$
(\cref{sec:validity-matched}). Headline numbers are reported at
canonical $\Lread{=}36$.

\subsection{Matched verb/length pools}
\label{sec:validity-matched}

We construct verb-class- and word-length-matched harmful/harmless
pools, re-extract $\dhatstar$, and compute per-layer
$\cos(\dhat, \dhatstar)$. A clean direction should produce a
high-cosine $\dhatstar$ on the matched pools.

\begin{decisionrule}[Matched pool]
\label{rule:matched}
$\cos(\dhat, \dhatstar) \geq 0.90$ at the canonical layer
$\Rightarrow$ clean. $\leq 0.70$ $\Rightarrow$ contaminated.
\end{decisionrule}

\paragraph{Result.}
$\cos(\dhat, \dhatstar) = 0.9748$ at canonical $\Lread{=}36$
(\texttt{refusal\_direction\_matched/compare\_to\_orig.json}).
The per-layer cosine sequence climbs from $0.69$ at $\layerL{=}0$ to a
plateau $\geq 0.97$ from $\layerL{=}19$ onward
(\cref{fig:exp4-cos}); canonical $\Lread{=}36$ sits well inside that
plateau. Comfortably above the clean threshold; $\dhat$ does not
collapse onto the verb-class / length confound.

\subsection{Style/vocabulary mismatch}
\label{sec:validity-style}

We construct an ``edgy harmless'' pool (benign tasks phrased with the
same lexical register as \advbench{}) and a ``camouflaged harmful''
pool (\advbench{} requests rewritten in mild, neutral language), and
project residuals onto $\dhat$. We compute two AUCs: $\mathrm{AUC}_{\text{intent}}$ ranks prompts by harm intent
(camouflaged-harmful as positive class), $\mathrm{AUC}_{\text{vocab}}$
ranks prompts by edgy-lexicon density.

\begin{decisionrule}[Style/vocabulary]
\label{rule:style}
$\mathrm{AUC}_{\text{intent}} \geq 0.85$ AND
$\mathrm{AUC}_{\text{vocab}} \in [0.35, 0.65]$ (preregistered chance
band).
\end{decisionrule}

\paragraph{Result.}
At canonical $\Lread{=}36$,
$\mathrm{AUC}_{\text{intent}} = 1.000$ and
$\mathrm{AUC}_{\text{vocab}} = 0.597$ on $\dhat$
(matched $\dhatstar$: $\mathrm{AUC}_{\text{vocab}} = 0.536$;
\texttt{validity/auc.json}). Both vocab AUCs sit inside the
preregistered chance band $[0.35, 0.65]$ while intent AUC saturates
at $1.000$. Verdict \textbf{intent-driven} (the direction reads prompt
meaning, not surface lexicon --- it fires on softly-phrased harm and
stays silent on edgy-tone benign prompts): projection tracks harmful
intent; edgy-vocab association is at chance
(\cref{fig:exp5-scatter}).

\subsection{Topic decoupling}
\label{sec:validity-topic}

We split prompts on two axes --- harm intent (harmful vs.\ harmless)
and topic tone (edgy vs.\ mundane) --- and ask whether $\dhat$ fires
on intent or on topic. For each prompt we project the residual onto
$\dhat$ and run a 2$\times$2 ANOVA, reporting partial $\eta^{2}$ for
each factor and their interaction.

\begin{decisionrule}[Topic decoupling]
\label{rule:topic}
Partial $\eta^{2}(\text{intent}) \geq 0.5$ AND
$\eta^{2}(\text{topic}) \leq 0.1$.
\end{decisionrule}

\paragraph{Result.}
At canonical $\Lread{=}36$ on $\dhat$,
$\eta^{2}(\text{intent}) = 0.963$, $\eta^{2}(\text{topic}) = 0.094$,
$\eta^{2}(\text{intent}{:}\text{topic}) = 0.002$
(\texttt{topic\_decouple/anova.json}). The intent effect is roughly
$10\times$ larger than the topic effect, and topic sits below the
preregistered $0.10$ ceiling: \textbf{passes} verdict on $\dhat$
(topic effect within the preregistered $\eta^{2}{\leq}0.1$ ceiling).
The matched-pool replication ($\dhatstar$) gives
$\eta^{2}(\text{intent}) = 0.957$, $\eta^{2}(\text{topic}) = 0.137$:
\textbf{partial pass} on the matched arm only (topic effect exceeds the
ceiling, though intent still dominates), triggering the preregistered
topic-residualisation check.

\paragraph{Robustness check.}
Within-topic discrimination is perfect on both pools:
$\mathrm{AUC}_{\text{intent}}{=}1.000$ on each topic stratum (mundane
and edgy) for $\dhat$ and $\dhatstar$. Regressing out the topic
label from each projection and re-running the ANOVA leaves
$\eta^{2}(\text{intent})$ unchanged at the headline value while
driving $\eta^{2}(\text{topic})$ below $10^{-30}$ on both arms
(\texttt{topic\_decouple/orthogonalisation.json}). The harm-intent
claim is therefore \textbf{robust} (intent effect survives the
preregistered topic-residualisation transform on both arms): $\dhat$
tracks topic-independent harm-intent, not a harmful-topic detector
(\cref{fig:exp6-topic-boxplot}; per-direction AUC scatter in
\cref{fig:exp6-topic-scatter}).

\subsection{Harm-refusal vs.\ policy-refusal AUC}
\label{sec:validity-policy}

We compare \advbench{}[50:100) (harm-refusal) against \sorrybench{}
\citep{xie2024sorrybench} categories 41--45 (policy-refusal: financial
advice, legal advice, etc.). A direction that tracks generic refusal
rather than harm should fail to separate these.

\begin{decisionrule}[Policy specificity]
\label{rule:policy}
$\mathrm{AUC}(\text{harm vs.\ policy}) \geq 0.85$.
\end{decisionrule}

\paragraph{Result.}
\Cref{tab:policy-aucs} reports the three pairwise AUCs at canonical
$\Lread{=}36$ on $\dhat$ and on the matched-pool replication
$\dhatstar$ (\texttt{policy\_validity/aucs.json}). The

\label{sec:mechanism}

$\Ncontext{=}0$ vs $\Ncontext{=}4096$ on \texttt{distractor}

full attention pattern to avoid OOM at long $\Ncontext$.

\midrule

The drop is attentional, not representational. $\dhat$ is extracted

tokens) is falsified. Consistent with \citet{zhao2025separately}:
harmfulness is encoded at the instruction tokens and refusal is
computed downstream by reading from them; softmax dilution cuts the
read. Per-head attribution heatmap and L36H31 source-token flow in
\cref{fig:phase6-attribution,fig:phase6-sourceflow}.

\section{Path Patching Localizes the Lesion to a Two-Head Circuit}
\label{sec:pathpatching}

We now ask \emph{which} earlier layer is responsible for L36H31's
attention loss. We use denoising path patching
\citep{goldowskydill2023patching} on $8$ held-out harmful prompts:
run the corrupted ($\Ncontext{=}512$ \texttt{distractor}) prompt, but
overwrite the residual stream at one chosen layer with the clean
($\Ncontext{=}0$) residual, only at the harmful-span tokens. We then
measure how much of the clean--dirty gap in the $\hat d$ projection at
L36's last token is restored. We call this fraction \emph{recovery}:
$0$ means the patch did nothing; $1$ means it fully fixed the lesion.

\subsection{Layer sweep: peak at $L=20$}

\Cref{tab:pp-layer,fig:phase7b-layer} report per-layer recovery (mean
$\pm$ SEM, $n{=}8$). Recovery is essentially zero in early layers
($\layerL{<}12$) and at the readout layer ($\layerL{\geq}28$), and
peaks at \textbf{$L=20$} with shoulders at $L=16$ and $L=24$. Patching at
$L=36$ itself does nothing---by then the lesion has already
propagated. The peak is not driven by a single prompt: dropping the
highest-recovery prompt still leaves $L=20$ the maximum
($6.6\% \pm 2.5\%$ SEM, $n{=}7$).

\subsection{Joint patching reveals a coupled cascade}

Patching $\{16, 20, 24\}$ together yields recovery $0.115 \pm 0.041$
SEM---better than the best single layer ($0.093$ at $L=20$) but worse
than the sum of the three single-layer recoveries ($0.178$). The
three layers therefore are not independent: clean residuals upstream
partly carry through to downstream layers, so single-layer patches
fix overlapping pieces of the same lesion
(\cref{fig:phase7b-multilayer}).

\subsection{The two-head mechanism}

Putting the three results together gives the circuit:

\begin{center}
\textbf{L20H10} $\to$ (mid-layer cascade) $\to$ \textbf{L36H31} $\to$ $\hat d$ write at L36's last token
\end{center}

Distractor bloat suppresses L20H10's read on the harmful tokens. That
deficit propagates subadditively through $L=16$--$24$, reaches L36H31,
and shows up as the $6\times$ attention-mass drop in
\cref{tab:phase6}. With L36H31 no longer writing $\hat d$ into the
residual, the L36 last-token state slides into the anti-refusal
half-space (\cref{sec:rescue}), and refusal collapses behaviorally.

\section{Output-Distribution Confirmation: Next-Token Refusal vs.\ Compliance Mass}
\label{sec:circuit-tracing}

To check that the head- and residual-level findings of
\cref{sec:mechanism,sec:rescue,sec:pathpatching} actually reach the
output distribution at the relevant token, we run a circuit-tracing
proxy harness on $4$ held-out harmful prompts under both the
\texttt{prefix} and \texttt{distractor} formats at $\Ncontext \in
\{0, 128, 512\}$. For each prompt we record (i) the proxy attention
mass from the final position to the harmful-span tokens at the
Guardrail Heads; (ii) the cosine of the L36 last-token residual onto
$\dhat$; and (iii) two probability sums over the next-token
distribution: $P_{\mathrm{ref}} = \sum_t P(t)$ for $t$ in a refusal
vocabulary (e.g., ``I'', ``Sorry'', ``I'm''), and
$P_{\mathrm{comp}} = \sum_t P(t)$ for $t$ in a compliance vocabulary
(e.g., ``Sure'', ``Here'', ``To'')\footnote{Full transcoder
attribution graphs (feature-level circuit attribution) were attempted
but exceeded the single-A100 memory budget; we report the
proxy-attention and next-token probability statistics that the same
harness logged in lieu of the feature graphs.}.

\Cref{tab:phase7-proxy,fig:phase7-proxy-attn} report the per-cell mean over $4$ prompts.
Two observations follow:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Compliance mass is format-conditional.} Under
        \texttt{distractor} at $\Ncontext{=}512$, $P_{\mathrm{comp}}$
        rises from $0.00$ (at $\Ncontext{=}0$) to $0.50$ --- i.e.,
        roughly half the next-token mass is on compliance starts.
        Under \texttt{prefix} at the same $\Ncontext$, $P_{\mathrm{comp}}$
        stays at $0.00$ across the entire grid. The output
        distribution flips into the compliance half-space only under
        \texttt{distractor}, mirroring the residual-level sign-flip
        of \cref{sec:rescue}.
  \item \textbf{Refusal mass tracks attention.} For both formats,
        $P_{\mathrm{ref}}$ scales monotonically with the proxy
        attention on harmful tokens: the more attention the
        Guardrail Heads place on the harmful span, the more refusal
        mass survives at the next-token distribution. The
        attentional$\to$behavioural pipeline is preserved at every
        layer of measurement.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{The failure is more specific than ``long context breaks
refusal.''}
The popular framing of long-context jailbreaks is that benign filler
dilutes attention generally and the safety prior loses to bulk. Our
results refine this in three ways. \emph{(i)~Format, not length.}
Five of six bloat formats at $\Ncontext{=}4096$ preserve refusal at
${\geq}87\%$. Only \texttt{distractor} breaks the model, and it does
so at ${\sim}31$ tokens. The relevant quantity is whether the prompt
structure produces \emph{competing tasks} that pull attention from the
harmful tokens. \emph{(ii)~Local in depth.} The lesion is at a single
mid-network band (L=16--24, peak L=20). By L=36 the damage is
irreversible; by L=12 the relevant computation has not yet happened.
\emph{(iii)~Local in head space.} A single attention head, L20H10,
accounts for the largest single-head contribution at the lesion
layer; the mechanism is a two-head circuit, not a diffuse
multi-component failure.

\paragraph{Why the single-direction rescue cannot work, and what could.}
The Phase-3 negative result is initially puzzling: $\dhat$ is a real,
narrow, causal direction (\cref{tab:phase1-validation,tab:capability}),
so why doesn't adding it back rescue refusal? The sign-flip finding
resolves this: the L36 residual at the last token does not merely lose
its $\dhat$ component; it acquires a \emph{negative} $\dhat$
component (mean projection $-58$ across prompts). Restoring the
refusal signal would require $\alpha$ an order of magnitude larger
than tested. Two viable interventions for future work follow:
larger or position-targeted $\alpha$ with a fine-grained capability
check; or \textbf{patching upstream at L20H10 directly} rather than
at the L36 residual --- targeting the cause rather than the symptom.

\paragraph{Implications for safety evaluation.}
If a single, structurally simple bloat format defeats refusal at
${\sim}31$ tokens, then short-context refusal benchmarks
\textbf{systematically overstate the safety margin of deployed
long-context models}. The gap is not narrow: \texttt{distractor}'s
intact-refusal-at-$\Ncontext{=}4096$ is $0.00$, against the same
prompts' baseline refusal of $0.97$. We recommend that long-context
safety evaluation explicitly include format-conditional dilution
with at least the distractor structure at multiple $\Ncontext$ values
($\{32, 128, 512, 4096\}$), and report the killer-comparison gap as a
single scalar of how attentionally fragile a model's refusal is.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{One model.} All experiments are on \model{}.
        Arditi-style refusal directions transfer across model
        families, but the specific Guardrail Heads (L20H10, L36H31)
        and the exact dilution slope almost certainly do not. The
        two-head circuit claim needs replication on at least one
        Llama-family and one Mistral-family safety-tuned model.
  \item \textbf{Small prompt sets.} $\dhat$ is extracted from $256$
        paired prompts; \cref{sec:multiformat} uses $100$ per cell;
        path patching uses $8$ per layer/head. The 8-prompt set is
        the limiting factor; joint and head-zoom recoveries have wide
        SEM bars.
  \item \textbf{Single bloat template per format.} Each of the six
        formats uses one canonical realization. Within-format
        variation in bloat content could shift slopes substantially.
  \item \textbf{Substring refusal detector.} Will mis-classify
        prefixed compliance (``I cannot help with that, but here is
        how$\ldots$'') as refusal and miss creative non-compliance.
        Model-judge or human-graded subset is needed before quoting
        the absolute refusal numbers as ground truth.
  \item \textbf{OOM-truncated long regime.} $\Ncontext \geq 8192$
        does not fit on a single A100-80GB with the current hook
        setup; longer-context rows are inferred indirectly via
        projection.
  \item \textbf{Two known eval-space overlaps} in \texttt{splits.json}
        (exp\_6 and exp\_7 both share \advbench{} rows with the
        exp\_8 sweep eval; flagged, not a $\dhat$-training leak).
  \item \textbf{Path patching only at the harmful-span tokens.}
        Patching all positions would likely yield higher recovery and
        may shift the localization. The $9\text{--}12\%$ recovery is
        therefore a \emph{lower bound} on the L=20 lesion's
        importance.
  \item \textbf{No mitigation evaluated.} LoRA-SFT and DPO
        mitigation training are left to future work; we discuss the
        proposed direction in \cref{sec:discussion}.
  \item \textbf{Greedy decoding only.} All generation is
        \texttt{do\_sample=False}; sampling-based jailbreak rates
        may differ.
\end{itemize}

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We localized the long-context jailbreak failure mode in \model{} to a
specific two-head circuit (\textbf{L20H10}\,$\to$\,\textbf{L36H31})
via a combination of difference-of-means refusal-direction extraction,
six-format dilution sweeps, attention-mass measurement,
capability-orthogonality auditing, and denoising path patching. The
failure is \textbf{attentional, not representational}; it is
\textbf{structure-conditional}, requiring task-competition formats
like \texttt{distractor}; it produces a \textbf{sign flip} in the
$\dhat$ projection that defeats single-site direction-level rescue;
and it originates at a \textbf{single mid-network attention head}
whose silencing cascades downstream to the dominant Guardrail Head at
the readoff layer. Three concrete extensions follow: replication on
Llama-family and Mistral-family safety-tuned models; feature-level
rescue via attribution graphs (transcoder support permitting); and
upstream intervention at L20H10 rather than at the L36 residual as a
more effective steering target.
